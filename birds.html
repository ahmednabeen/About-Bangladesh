Birds of Bangladesh

The birds of Bangladesh are living jewels of its natural landscape, filling the air with vibrant colors, enchanting melodies, and a sense of harmony that echoes the country's deep-rooted connection with nature. At the heart of this avian symphony is the national bird, the Oriental Magpie-Robin—locally known as the Doyel—celebrated for its sweet, flute-like song and seen as a symbol of joy, resilience, and the poetic spirit of the Bangladeshi people. From the misty forests of Sylhet and the hilly terrains of Chittagong to the vast wetlands of Hakaluki Haor and the mysterious Sundarbans, more than 700 bird species grace the skies, trees, and rivers. Graceful Kingfishers dive into clear waters, vibrant Green Bee-eaters dart through the air, and elegant Openbill Storks wade through marshes, each playing a crucial role in pollination, seed dispersal, and natural pest control. During Winter, the arrival of migratory guests like Siberian ducks and bar-headed geese transforms the wetlands into magical landscapes of fluttering wings and echoing calls. Birds such as the regal Brahminy Kite or the iridescent Purple Sunbird aren’t just ecological assets—they are muses of art, folklore, and song, embodying beauty and freedom. In rural life, birds serve as natural clocks and seasonal markers, binding everyday routines to the rhythm of nature. Celebrations like bird festivals and conservation efforts reflect a growing awareness to protect these winged wonders from habitat loss and environmental threats. Altogether, the birds of Bangladesh are more than just creatures of flight—they are symbols of balance, beauty, and the vibrant soul of a nation where nature and culture soar together.

Oriental Magpie-Robin

The Oriental Magpie-Robin, or Doyel, Bangladesh’s national bird, is a striking black-and-white songbird with a melodious, flute-like call, symbolizing joy and resilience. Found in gardens, forests, and urban areas across Sylhet to Chittagong, it thrives in diverse habitats. It feeds on insects, worms, and small fruits, often foraging on the ground. Known for its bold personality and territorial displays, the Doyel is a skilled mimic, enchanting listeners with varied songs. These birds typically live 3-5 years in the wild. Conservation efforts protect them from habitat loss, ensuring their vibrant presence endures.

House Sparrow

The House Sparrow, known as "Charai" in Bangladesh, is a small, lively bird common across urban and rural areas. It thrives in human settlements, nesting in roof crevices, trees, or building gaps. Feeding on seeds, grains, insects, and food scraps, it adapts easily to diverse environments. Its chirpy calls and social nature make it a familiar sight, often seen in flocks. House Sparrows are resilient, with a lifespan of 3-5 years, though some live longer. Their adaptability and close association with human habitats make them a cherished part of Bangladesh’s avian landscape, despite declining populations globally.

Black Francolin

The Black Francolin (Francolinus francolinus), a rare resident of Bangladesh, is a medium-sized gamebird in the pheasant family, known locally as kaala teetar. Found in tall grasses, farmlands, and scrublands near water in Chittagong, Dhaka, and Rajshahi divisions, it forages on seeds, grains, shoots, berries, and insects like termites. This shy, terrestrial bird is active at dawn and dusk, producing a distinctive “cheek-keraykek” call. Monogamous, it nests in ground depressions, laying 6-9 eggs. Its lifespan is around 5-8 years. Threats include habitat loss and hunting.

Common Myna

The Common Myna (Acridotheres tristis), known as Shalik in Bangladesh, is a widespread bird thriving in urban areas, farmlands, and open woodlands. Omnivorous, it eats insects, fruits, seeds, and human food scraps, often foraging on the ground. Its distinctive brown body, black head, and yellow beak make it recognizable. Noted for mimicking human speech and varied vocalizations, it’s a skilled communicator. Mynas are social, roosting in large flocks and nesting in tree holes or buildings. They live about 4 years in the wild, sometimes reaching 12. Their adaptability makes them a common sight across Bangladesh.

The Green Peafowl

The Green Peafowl (Pavo muticus), once found in southeastern Bangladesh, is now extinct there due to habitat loss and hunting. These large birds inhabit tropical forests, grasslands, and farmland edges, preferring areas near water. They are omnivorous, eating fruits, invertebrates, small reptiles, and plants. Known for their iridescent bluish-green plumage and long tails, both sexes look similar, unlike Indian Peafowl. They roost in trees at night and forage on the ground during the day. Their lifespan averages 15 years in the wild, longer in captivity. Conservation efforts are crucial for this endangered species.

The White-bellied Sea Eagle

The White-bellied Sea Eagle, a majestic bird of prey in Bangladesh, inhabits coastal areas, estuaries, and inland wetlands like those in the Sundarbans. It primarily feeds on fish, sea snakes, and occasionally birds and mammals, often stealing food from other birds. This opportunistic piscivore uses its sharp, curved talons to snatch prey from water. With a wingspan up to 2.2 meters, it soars over waterways, nesting in tall trees or cliffs. It lives up to 30 years, breeding in monogamous pairs. Though globally Least Concern, habitat loss threatens its population in Bangladesh.

The White-breasted Waterhen

The White-breasted Waterhen (Amaurornis phoenicurus), known as "Dahuk" in Bangladesh, is a common waterbird found in marshes, ponds, and ditches. It inhabits wetlands, rice fields, and gardens, often near water, and is less secretive than other rails. Its diet includes insects, small fish, mollusks, seeds, and vegetable matter. This bird is known for loud, croaky calls during the breeding season (June-October). It builds shallow nests in marsh vegetation, laying 6-7 eggs. The White-breasted Waterhen is monogamous and adaptable, with a lifespan of about 5-10 years. Its population is stable, listed as Least Concern.

Brown Hawl Owl

The Brown Hawk-Owl (Ninox scutulata), also known as the Brown Boobook, is a resident bird in Bangladesh’s forests and well-wooded areas, often near water or human settlements. This medium-sized owl, about 30-36 cm long, is nocturnal, roosting in shady branches during the day. It feeds on large insects, frogs, lizards, small birds, and mice, hunting from exposed perches at dusk. Its distinctive call is a soft, musical “oo-uk…ooo-uk.” It nests in tree holes, laying 3-5 eggs. The species is adaptable, with a lifespan of about 10-15 years. Its conservation status is Least Concern.

Baya Weaver

The Baya Weaver (Ploceus philippinus), a small bird native to Bangladesh, is renowned for its intricately woven, retort-shaped nests built by males during the monsoon season. These sparrow-sized birds live in flocks in grasslands, farmlands, and wetlands, often nesting on thorny trees or over water to avoid predators. Their diet is mainly granivorous, consisting of seeds and grains, supplemented by insects, small frogs, and mollusks. Known for their polygynous mating, males build multiple nests to attract females. Their lifespan is approximately 10-15 years, though habitat loss threatens in Bangladesh.

White-rumped Shama

The White-rumped Shama (Copsychus malabaricus), a melodious songbird, thrives in Bangladesh’s forests and woodlands. Preferring dense undergrowth, it avoids human settlements, favoring shady thickets. This shy, territorial bird feeds mainly on insects like ants, worms, and caterpillars, foraging on the ground or in bushes. Its standout feature is its rich, flute-like song, often mimicking other birds, making it a favorite among bird enthusiasts. Males are glossy black with a white rump and chestnut belly; females are greyish-brown. Nesting from April to June, it lays 3-4 eggs. Its lifespan is around 7-10 years.

Black Drongo

The Black Drongo (Dicrurus macrocercus), known as "Finge" in Bangladesh, is a small, glossy black bird with a distinctive forked tail. It inhabits open fields, savannas, and urban areas, often perching on wires or bushes to hunt. Primarily insectivorous, it feeds on grasshoppers, beetles, and bees, catching them mid-air or gleaning from the ground. Its aggressive nature, earning it the name "king crow," involves dive-bombing larger birds to protect its territory. Black Drongos live about 10-12 years and are playful, sometimes dropping leaves to catch them mid-air. They are common, with no major threats.

Mountain Bamboo-Partridge

The Mountain Bamboo-Partridge (Bambusicola fytchii), a small bird in the Phasianidae family, inhabits Bangladesh’s bamboo scrub forests, tall grasslands, and degraded forest areas near water. It stays under shrub cover most of the day, foraging for seeds, berries, shoots, buds, grains, insects, and worms in early morning and late evening. Its plumage, a mix of browns, cream, and grey with black-and-white eye stripes, provides excellent camouflage. Breeding occurs from March to September, with females eggs for 18-19 days in simple ground nests. The species, fairly common but declining, lives about 5-7 years.

Siberian ducks(Guest Bird)

Every winter, Siberian ducks migrate to warmer regions, becoming special seasonal guests in many parts of South Asia. These elegant waterfowl travel thousands of kilometers to escape the harsh Siberian cold, settling near wetlands, rivers, and lakes. Their arrival brings life and color to the otherwise quiet water bodies, delighting bird watchers and nature lovers alike. The presence of these ducks is not only a natural spectacle but also a reminder of the importance of conserving habitats. As temporary visitors, they symbolize harmony between nature and wildlife, enriching the biodiversity of their host regions during the chilly winter months.

The Bar-Headed Goose

The bar-headed goose is a remarkable bird known for its incredible high-altitude migrations over the Himalayas. Native to Central Asia, these geese can fly at altitudes over 7,000 meters, where oxygen levels are dangerously low. Their powerful lungs, efficient oxygen usage, and unique hemoglobin allow them to survive such extreme conditions. Recognized by the two distinctive black bars on their head, they migrate from their wintering grounds in India to breeding sites in Tibet and Central Asia. Bar-headed geese feed on grasses and grains and are often found near lakes and wetlands. Their resilience makes them a marvel of nature.

Herons

Herons are long-legged, freshwater and coastal birds known for their graceful appearance and patient hunting skills. Belonging to the family Ardeidae, they are found on every continent except Antarctica. These birds typically have long necks and sharp beaks, which they use to catch fish, frogs, and small aquatic animals. Herons are often seen standing still in shallow water, waiting to strike swiftly at their prey. They build large nests in trees, often in colonies called heronries. Species like the great blue heron and the grey heron are well-known. Herons play a vital role in maintaining healthy wetland ecosystems.

The Black-tailed Godwit

The Black-tailed Godwit (Limosa limosa) is a large, elegant wading bird known for its long legs and straight bill. It has a striking appearance, with a mix of grey, white, and black plumage in winter, and rich orange-brown tones during breeding season. Native to Europe and Asia, it migrates to Africa, South Asia, and Australia in winter. This species prefers wetlands, marshes, and estuaries for feeding on insects, worms, and small invertebrates. Its numbers have declined due to habitat loss, making it a near-threatened species. Conservation efforts are crucial to ensure its survival and protect its breeding grounds.

In the verdant realm of Bangladesh, where avian choristers like the Mountain Bamboo-Partridge and the resplendent White-throated Kingfisher reign, birds are the lifeblood of nature’s grand tapestry. Their diligent pollination, seed dispersal, and mastery over pests uphold the delicate balance of forest, marsh, and meadow. Over seven hundred species, joined by transient guests like the Black-tailed Godwit, adorn the land from Chittagong’s rugged hills to the coastal mangrove cathedrals. These winged muses inspire timeless verse and lore, yet face perils of vanishing groves. Through ardent conservation, their celestial dance endures, gracing Bangladesh’s eternal skies.
